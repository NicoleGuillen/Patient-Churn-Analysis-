# Patient-Churn-Analysis-
An insight look of a Google Sheet analysis from a dataset including Patient churn information to realize what is needed to support the satisfaction of the patients and their needs depending their age range.

Introduction and Objective: In order to have financial stability in any healthcare facility, patients needs to have the best care possible because they are the human beings in need of attention and they are the customers of the health sector that nopurishes the business revenue. As defined by The Weave Resource Center ''Patient churn refers to the phenomenon where a dissatisfied patient cancels their appointment or chooses another healthcare provider over your organization. Customer churn affects your practice’s finances, reputation, and ability to provide medical services to other patients in need.'' The objective becomes understanding the causes of the patient churn to provide a balanced and stable flow for the patients and the healthcare facility to ensure an excelent reputation as a healthcare facility where patients will come by the recommendation of others as a confident a certified place to be attended and this will cause a consistent revenue that will help the business to grow.

Main issues found in the analysis: 
1. Churn Rate Analysis by Insurance Type:
No Insurance (None): Shows the highest churn rate at 27.33% (41 dropouts out of a total of 150 patients).
Government Insurance: It follows closely with a dropout rate of 26.11% (47 dropouts out of a total of 180 patients).
Private Insurance: This is the most stable segment, recording the lowest dropout rate at 21.76% (37 dropouts out of a total of 170 patients).


2. Analysis by Age Group and Insurance Type
A. Missed Appointments 
The cumulative volume of missed appointments totals 2,211 and serves as a critical indicator of patient disengagement:
Ages 36–60: The 36–60 age group accounts for the highest number of missed appointments, with 925 no-shows. Within this group, private and government insurance plans are the most affected (331 and 322 missed appointments, respectively).
Young Adults (Ages 19–35): This is the segment with the second-highest number of missed appointments, totaling 582. Government insurance accounts for the largest share of this issue within the segment, with 264 missed appointments.
Older adults (61 years or older): 663 missed appointments were recorded, concentrated primarily among private insurance holders (271).
Insight: The 2,211 missed appointments are not merely random oversights; they represent the primary symptom of attrition. The 36–60 age group accounts for nearly 42% of these absences (925 appointments). This indicates that individuals in their peak working years face time constraints or scheduling conflicts that force them to miss appointments, thereby breaking the habit of attendance and leading to churn.
Insight: Patients with "No Insurance" (None) have the highest dropout rate, at 27.33%. This indicates that the current model lacks financial flexibility. Without a third party (insurer) to buffer costs, the patient views medical care as a "heavy expense" rather than an "investment in health," abandoning treatment at the first sign of apparent recovery or financial hardship.

B. Satisfaction Score
The overall average satisfaction score is 3.05 out of 5, indicating that the patient experience is fair and there is significant room for improvement:
The Most Satisfied: Young patients (19–35) under a government scheme report the highest average at 3.35 (and a median of 3.4). Although the number of missed appointments is significant (264), they are satisfied with the service received. This indicates that they are loyal to the medical care itself, but the issues are logistical.
The Least Satisfied: The lowest average is found among children and adolescents (0–18) with government insurance (2.76), followed by older adults (61+) who do not have health insurance (2.86).

Strategic Recommendations:
Short Term: Mitigating Missed Appointments Among Adults (Aged 36–60): Given that this group accounts for 925 missed appointments, it is advisable to implement an automated reminder system (via WhatsApp, SMS, iMessage, or phone call) providing 48- and 24-hour advance notice. This will reduce absenteeism and prevent patients from losing interest and dropping out of the service.

Medium Term: Flexible Corporate Scheduling and Telemedicine (for the 36–60 age group): Extended service hours (after 6:00 PM or on weekends) will be implemented, and a telemedicine platform will be made available for follow-up consultations that do not require an in-person visit, should the client prefer this option.This measure is being implemented to directly address the issue of 925 missed appointments within the adult demographic, enabling them to attend to their health needs without compromising their work schedules.

Long term: 
Compliance Incentives for Young Adults (Ages 19–35): We should capitalize on this demographic's high satisfaction levels by using gamification within a mobile app. For every appointment attended on time, users earn points redeemable at pharmacies or for wellness services (such as nutrition counseling or psychological support). This approach aims to reduce the 264 missed appointments through positive reinforcement. This is a long-term initiative, as it requires securing partnerships with pharmacies and wellness providers willing to collaborate on the campaign, as well as developing the mobile app with qualified professionals.

Prepaid Preventive Health Program (for the uninsured segment): It can be designed a low-cost monthly or quarterly subscription package covering basic consultations and preventive screenings.
The goal is to spread the cost of consultations over a membership model, thereby lowering the barrier of direct, per-visit payments. This encourages the 27.33% of uninsured patients to maintain continuity of care without the hassles associated with insurance companies or excessive paperwork. Customers can cancel the membership by visiting the clinic or using the mobile app.
